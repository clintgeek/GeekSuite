# GuitarGeek Lesson Writing Style & Conventions

## Tone & Voice

- Friendly, encouraging, and accessible
- Direct and conversational (uses "you", "your", "let's")
- Uses parenthetical asides for emphasis or clarification
- Practical, beginner-focused
- Avoids jargon without explanation

## Structure Patterns

Each lesson has 5-10 steps that progressively build understanding:

1. **Why/What Step**: Explains the concept, purpose, or "aha!" moment
2. **How Step**: Practical finger placement, technique, or mechanics
3. **Sound/Feel Step**: Trains ear and muscle memory ("listen to...", "feel...")
4. **Application Step**: Real song contexts or progressions
5. **Practice Step**: Specific, time-bound practice exercises

## Content Strategies from Current Lessons

### Explanations

- Use analogies: "like the number 1", "like waving goodbye with your wrist"
- Connect to music: blues, songs, emotional quality ("sounds happy/sad")
- Show relationships: "like D major, only..."
- Use emojis sparingly for symbols: ↓ ↑

### Motivation/Context

- "This unlocks countless songs"
- "You'll be able to start playing immediately"
- Mention real-world applications (blues, folk, rock)
- Acknowledge difficulty honestly but show paths forward

### Hands-On Details

- Specific finger numbers: "INDEX finger on 1st fret..."
- Specific string references: "high E string", "thick string (6th)"
- Step-by-step progression
- Visual asset references when available

### Tips & Tricks

- Separate "tricks" into memorable hooks
- Use memory aids: shape associations, finger patterns
- Address common mistakes directly
- Provide workarounds when perfect form isn't possible

## DO NOT Include from Friend's Content

- Long philosophical sections (even if insightful)
- Lengthy theory discussions beyond beginner scope
- Overly academic language
- Multiple paragraphs without practical action items

## DO Extract from Friend's Content

- Memory hooks and shape associations (A looks like 1, E like lowercase e, D like D)
- Bass note rules (lowest note determines chord type)
- Major vs minor emotional quality
- Finger positioning tips and hand angles
- Common beginner mistakes
- Progression examples
- Related chords (Am follows E major shape, Em follows E major by removing a finger)
